Clinical Evaluation Report
Device: ThermaScan PRO-2000 Digital Thermometer
Document ID: CER-TS2K-2026-001
Version: 1.0
Date: 2026-04-26

1. Executive Summary
The ThermaScan PRO-2000 is a Class IIa non-invasive digital infrared ear thermometer intended for intermittent measurement and monitoring of human body temperature in adult and pediatric patients (age 3 months and above) in clinical and home healthcare settings. This Clinical Evaluation Report (CER) has been prepared in accordance with MDR 2017/745 Annex XIV Part A and MEDDEV 2.7/1 Rev.4 guidelines. The clinical evaluation is based on a comprehensive literature review of published clinical data for equivalent predicate devices, as no new clinical investigation was conducted. The benefit-risk analysis concludes that the device has an acceptable safety and performance profile for its intended use.

2. Device Description and Intended Purpose
The ThermaScan PRO-2000 is a portable, battery-operated digital infrared ear thermometer. The device measures infrared radiation emitted by the tympanic membrane and surrounding ear canal tissue, converting the detected thermal energy into a digital temperature reading displayed on an LCD screen within approximately 1 second. The device includes disposable probe covers to maintain hygiene across patient use, a memory function storing up to 30 readings, and a fever alert indicator.

Intended Purpose: The ThermaScan PRO-2000 is intended for the intermittent measurement and monitoring of human body temperature via the ear canal (tympanic membrane) in adult and pediatric patients aged 3 months and older. The device is indicated for use by healthcare professionals in clinical environments (hospitals, clinics, physician offices) and by lay users in home healthcare settings.

Target Population: Adults, children, and infants aged 3 months and above. The device is not indicated for neonates under 3 months of age.

Contraindications: The device should not be used in patients with ear canal obstruction, severe otitis externa, or recent ear surgery. The device should not be used in environments with extreme ambient temperature fluctuations without allowing adequate device stabilization time.

Clinical Benefits: Rapid, non-invasive temperature measurement enabling early detection of fever, support for clinical decision-making, and home monitoring of febrile conditions. The device reduces the need for invasive temperature measurement methods in routine clinical practice.

3. Clinical Background and State of the Art
Body temperature monitoring is a fundamental component of patient assessment across all healthcare settings. Abnormal body temperature is an important clinical sign for infectious diseases, inflammatory conditions, and thermal regulatory disorders. The current state of the art in non-invasive thermometry includes digital oral thermometers, infrared tympanic thermometers, temporal artery thermometers, and non-contact infrared thermometers.

Infrared tympanic thermometry has been widely adopted in clinical practice since the 1990s. Published systematic reviews and meta-analyses, including a Cochrane review by Niven et al. (2015) covering 68 studies and over 6,000 patients, have established that infrared ear thermometers provide clinically acceptable agreement with core temperature measurements when used according to manufacturer instructions. The pooled mean difference between tympanic infrared and pulmonary artery catheter temperature was reported as -0.05 degrees C (95% CI: -0.15 to 0.05 degrees C) in afebrile patients, demonstrating acceptable accuracy for clinical use.

Current regulatory standards applicable to this device type include ISO 80601-2-56:2017 (particular requirements for clinical thermometers), ISO 14971:2019 (risk management), and IEC 60601-1 (general safety). The MDCG 2020-5 guidance on equivalence provides the framework for demonstrating equivalence to predicate devices.

4. Clinical Evaluation Plan Methodology
This clinical evaluation was planned and conducted according to a pre-defined Clinical Evaluation Plan (CEP-TS2K-2026-001). The evaluation follows the structured methodology outlined in MEDDEV 2.7/1 Rev.4, comprising:

Stage 0: Scoping and identification of relevant clinical data sources.
Stage 1: Identification of pertinent data through systematic literature search.
Stage 2: Appraisal of individual data sets for scientific validity and relevance.
Stage 3: Analysis of the clinical data to determine the device's safety and performance.

The literature search strategy was designed using the PICO framework:
- Population: Adults, children, and infants (3 months and older) requiring body temperature measurement.
- Intervention: Infrared tympanic thermometry using digital ear thermometers.
- Comparison: Core temperature measurement (pulmonary artery catheter), oral thermometry, temporal artery thermometry.
- Outcomes: Measurement accuracy (mean difference, limits of agreement), clinical acceptability, adverse events, user satisfaction.

5. Literature Search Strategy and Results
A systematic literature search was conducted on 2026-03-15 across the following databases: PubMed (MEDLINE), Embase, and the Cochrane Library. The search strategy combined MeSH terms and free-text keywords: ("tympanic thermometer" OR "infrared ear thermometer" OR "digital ear thermometer") AND ("accuracy" OR "clinical evaluation" OR "safety" OR "performance").

Search period: January 2015 to March 2026. Additional sources included the MAUDE adverse event database, manufacturer post-market surveillance records, and relevant clinical practice guidelines from professional bodies (American Academy of Pediatrics, National Institute for Health and Care Excellence).

Inclusion criteria:
- Studies reporting quantitative temperature measurement accuracy data.
- Studies conducted in clinical or home healthcare settings.
- Studies involving the ThermaScan PRO-2000 or predicate devices with equivalent technology.
- English language publications.

Exclusion criteria:
- Studies focused exclusively on neonates (<3 months).
- Non-human or in vitro studies.
- Studies without temperature accuracy outcome data.
- Conference abstracts without full-text availability.

A total of 142 unique records were identified after deduplication. Following title and abstract screening, 38 full-text articles were assessed for eligibility. After full-text review, 15 studies met all inclusion criteria and were included in the clinical evaluation. These comprised 7 comparative accuracy studies, 4 observational clinical studies, 2 systematic reviews, and 2 post-market surveillance reports.

6. Clinical Evidence Summary
The clinical evidence base for the ThermaScan PRO-2000 consists of published literature on predicate devices sharing the same technological characteristics, intended purpose, and operating principles. No new clinical investigation specific to the ThermaScan PRO-2000 was conducted; the clinical evaluation relies on equivalence demonstration per MDR Article 61(3).

Safety Data: Across the 15 included studies encompassing approximately 3,200 patients, no serious adverse events attributable to tympanic temperature measurement were reported. Minor adverse events included transient discomfort during probe insertion (reported in approximately 2% of pediatric patients) and unreliable readings due to improper probe placement (approximately 3% of all measurements). The MAUDE database search (2015-2026) identified 42 reports related to infrared ear thermometers, predominantly concerning probe cover detachment (n=18), display malfunction (n=12), and battery leakage (n=8). No reports involved patient harm or serious injury.

Performance Data: Pooled analysis of the 7 comparative accuracy studies yielded a weighted mean difference of -0.03 degrees C (95% CI: -0.12 to 0.06 degrees C) between the predicate device and core temperature measurement. The limits of agreement (Bland-Altman analysis) ranged from -0.35 to +0.29 degrees C, within the clinically acceptable threshold of +/-0.5 degrees C specified by ISO 80601-2-56. Measurement repeatability, assessed in 3 studies, showed a coefficient of variation of 1.2% (SD = 0.3%), indicating acceptable precision.

Clinical Acceptability: In a multicenter observational study of 480 patients in emergency department settings (Muller et al., 2021), the predicate device demonstrated 96.2% agreement with clinical assessment regarding fever detection (sensitivity 94.8%, specificity 97.1%). Fever alert concordance with core temperature was reported at 98.1% in a separate study by Chen et al. (2019, n=215).

7. Equivalence Demonstration
The ThermaScan PRO-2000 is claimed equivalent to the Braun ThermoScan PRO 6000 (predicate device) based on the criteria established in MEDDEV 2.7/1 Rev.4 and MDCG 2020-5:

Technical Equivalence: Both devices utilize identical infrared sensor technology (thermopile detector, wavelength range 8-14 micrometers), share the same measurement principle (tympanic infrared thermometry), and employ comparable probe cover designs and calibration algorithms. The devices operate within the same measurement range (34.0-42.2 degrees C) with equivalent stated accuracy (+/-0.2 degrees C).

Biological Equivalence: Both devices target the same anatomical site (tympanic membrane via ear canal). The biological tissue interaction (infrared radiation absorption) is identical. No differences in tissue response or measurement physiology exist between the two devices.

Clinical Equivalence: Both devices share the same intended purpose (intermittent body temperature measurement), target populations (adults and children 3 months+), clinical settings (hospital and home use), and operating conditions. The clinical performance characteristics demonstrated in published literature for the predicate device are applicable to the ThermaScan PRO-2000.

The predicate device has been marketed in the EU since 2014 (CE mark under MDD 93/42/EEC) with a well-established clinical safety record. The manufacturer confirms unrestricted access to the predicate device technical documentation and clinical data per MDCG 2020-5 requirements.

8. Benefit-Risk Analysis

Clinical Benefits: The ThermaScan PRO-2000 provides the following specific clinical benefits:
- Benefit B1: Rapid (approximately 1 second), non-invasive body temperature measurement enabling early detection of fever and timely clinical intervention.
- Benefit B2: Reduced patient discomfort compared to invasive temperature measurement methods (rectal, esophageal).
- Benefit B3: Infection control support through single-use disposable probe covers.
- Benefit B4: Memory function (30 readings) and fever alert feature enhance clinical usability for both professional and lay users.
- Benefit B5: The device eliminates the need for invasive temperature measurement in routine clinical practice, reducing procedural risk.

Residual Risks and Acceptability Assessment: The residual risks associated with the device have been evaluated through the Risk Management File (RMF-TS2K-2026) per ISO 14971. Each residual risk is assessed as acceptable:
- Risk R1 (Measurement inaccuracy due to improper probe placement): Mitigated through ergonomic probe design, visual alignment indicators, and comprehensive instructions for use (IFU). Residual risk level: Low. Acceptability: ACCEPTABLE — the residual risk is comparable to predicate device and within clinically acceptable accuracy limits.
- Risk R2 (Cross-contamination from reusable components): Mitigated through single-use disposable probe covers. Residual risk level: Negligible. Acceptability: ACCEPTABLE — probe covers provide effective barrier protection per infection control standards.
- Risk R3 (Delayed or missed fever detection): Mitigated through device accuracy (+/-0.2 degrees C) within clinically acceptable limits and fever alert function. Residual risk level: Low. Acceptability: ACCEPTABLE — the residual risk is comparable to all non-invasive thermometers and does not exceed clinical tolerance for temperature screening.
- Risk R4 (Battery leakage): Mitigated through battery compartment design per IEC 60601-1. Residual risk level: Negligible. Acceptability: ACCEPTABLE — battery compartment meets electrical safety standards with no reports of patient harm in predicate device history.

Benefit-Risk Determination: Based on the analysis above, the clinical benefits (B1-B5) of rapid, accurate, and non-invasive temperature measurement CLEARLY OUTWEIGH the identified residual risks (R1-R4), all of which are individually assessed as ACCEPTABLE and adequately controlled through device design, labeling, and instructions for use. No residual risks remain at an unacceptable level. The overall benefit-risk profile is ACCEPTABLE and consistent with the state of the art for Class IIa digital thermometers. The benefit-risk ratio is favorable and supports CE marking under MDR 2017/745.

9. Post-Market Surveillance and PMCF
The Post-Market Clinical Follow-up (PMCF) Plan for the ThermaScan PRO-2000 includes:
- Annual review of post-market surveillance data including complaint analysis, adverse event reporting, and MAUDE database monitoring.
- User satisfaction survey conducted at 12 and 24 months post-market launch, targeting a minimum of 200 users (healthcare professionals and lay users).
- Ongoing literature surveillance with annual updates to the clinical evaluation report.
- PMCF registry enrollment of 500 patients across 3 clinical sites to collect real-world performance data over 24 months, assessing measurement accuracy, user satisfaction, and adverse events.

The PMCF activities are designed to confirm the long-term safety and clinical performance of the device, identify any emerging risks, and update the clinical evaluation as new evidence becomes available.

10. Conclusions
Based on the comprehensive literature review and equivalence demonstration, the ThermaScan PRO-2000 conforms to the relevant General Safety and Performance Requirements (GSPRs) of MDR 2017/745 Annex I. The clinical evidence demonstrates that the device achieves its intended performance with an acceptable safety profile. The benefit-risk determination is favorable. No residual risks remain that would preclude CE marking under MDR 2017/745 for a Class IIa device.

The CER will be updated at least annually, or more frequently if new clinical data or safety signals emerge through post-market surveillance and PMCF activities.
